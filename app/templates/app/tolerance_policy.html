Tolerance Policy

Our approach to plagiarism detection and similarity thresholds

Overview

Scholar Indexing System (S.I.S) maintains a comprehensive tolerance policy for plagiarism detection that balances academic integrity with practical considerations. Our policy recognizes that some similarity is inevitable in academic writing, particularly in methodology sections, common phrases, and properly cited references.

We use advanced plagiarism detection tools, including Turnitin, to assess the originality of submitted work. Our tolerance levels are designed to identify genuine plagiarism while allowing for legitimate academic practices.

Similarity Thresholds

Low Risk (0-15%)

0-15%

Acceptable similarity level. This range typically includes common phrases, methodology descriptions, and properly cited references. No action required.

Medium Risk (16-30%)

16-30%

Requires review. May include legitimate similarities but should be examined to ensure proper citation and attribution. Minor revisions may be recommended.

High Risk (31%+)

31%+

Requires immediate attention. Significant similarity detected that may indicate plagiarism. Detailed review and potential rejection unless properly justified.

Exclusions from Similarity Check

Automatically Excluded

  • Quotes: Properly formatted quotations with citations are excluded from similarity calculations.
  • Bibliography/References: Reference lists and bibliographies are automatically excluded.
  • Common Phrases: Standard academic phrases and terminology common to the field.
  • Methodology Sections: Standard methodology descriptions may show similarity but are evaluated contextually.
  • Small Matches: Matches of less than 5 consecutive words are typically ignored.

Contextual Evaluation

We recognize that similarity percentages alone do not tell the full story. Our review process considers:

  • Source Attribution: Whether similar content is properly cited and attributed to original sources.
  • Field-Specific Norms: Some fields have standard terminology and methodologies that naturally result in higher similarity.
  • Self-Citation: Citations of the author's own previous work are evaluated differently from external sources.
  • Common Knowledge: Widely accepted facts and common knowledge may show similarity without indicating plagiarism.
  • Translation Issues: Works translated from other languages may show similarity to source materials, which is acceptable with proper attribution.

Action Guidelines

For Submissions with 0-15% Similarity

No action required. Submissions are automatically accepted for indexing.

For Submissions with 16-30% Similarity

  • Review team examines the similarity report
  • Verifies proper citation and attribution
  • May request minor revisions if citation issues are found
  • Submissions with proper attribution are typically accepted

For Submissions with 31%+ Similarity

  • Comprehensive review by senior editorial team
  • Detailed analysis of similarity sources
  • Requires justification and explanation from author
  • May require significant revisions or result in rejection
  • Repeated high similarity may result in account restrictions

Appeals Process

If your submission is flagged for high similarity, you have the right to appeal the decision. To appeal:

  • Provide detailed explanation of the similarities
  • Submit evidence of proper attribution and citation
  • Explain any field-specific norms that may account for similarity
  • Request a manual review by our editorial team

Appeals are reviewed within 5-7 business days, and you will receive a detailed response explaining the final decision.

Important: Our tolerance policy is designed to maintain academic integrity while being fair to researchers. We encourage all authors to ensure proper citation and attribution in their work. When in doubt, cite your sources.

Zero Tolerance for

  • Direct Copying: Verbatim copying of text without quotation marks and citation
  • Paraphrasing Plagiarism: Rewording others' work without proper attribution
  • Self-Plagiarism: Reusing substantial portions of your own previously published work without disclosure
  • Fabrication: Creating false data or citations
  • Multiple Submissions: Submitting the same work to multiple platforms without disclosure
